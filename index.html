Physical Examination of the Neck (Ø§Ù„ÙØ­Øµ Ø§Ù„Ø¬Ø³Ø¯ÙŠ Ù„Ù„Ø±Ù‚Ø¨Ø©)

Inspection:

  • Inspect the neck for symmetry, masses, and enlargement of gland and lymph nodes.
  • Normal findings: Symmetric with head centered and without bulging masses.
  • Thyroid gland moves upward when swallowing.
  • Abnormal findings: Swelling, enlarged masses, or nodules may indicate an enlarged thyroid gland, inflammation of lymph nodes, or a tumor.

Palpation:

  • Palpate the thyroid gland for size, shape, symmetry, tenderness, and nodules.
  • Normal findings: The thyroid gland is not palpable. The isthmus is the only portion of the thyroid that is normally palpable.
  • Abnormal findings: An enlarged, tender gland may result from thyroiditis.

  • If enlargement of the thyroid gland is detected, auscultate the area over the gland for a bruit.
  • Normal findings: No bruit should be heard.
  • Abnormal findings: A bruit (vibrations sound of blood flow through arteries) may be heard with the diaphragm of the stethoscope in an enlarged gland.

Introduction:

The eye transmits visual stimuli to the brain for interpretation and, in doing so, functions as the organ of vision. To perform a thorough assessment of the eye, the nurse should have a good understanding of the external structure of the eye, the visual fields, and the visual reflexes.

Health History:

  • Ask about history of previous eye surgery, trauma, use of corrective glasses or contact lenses
  • Blurred vision, diplopia, recent changes in vision, date of previous vision test
  • Allergies, eye redness, frequent watering or discharge
  • Health-related factors such as increased blood pressure or diabetes mellitus

Equipment:

  • Eye chart (Snellen chart)
  • Cover card
  • Penlight
  • Ophthalmoscope

Physical Examination:

  • Position and alignment: Eyeballs should be symmetrically aligned in sockets without protruding or sinking. Abnormal findings include exophthalmos (protruding eyeballs) or sunken eyeballs (seen with severe dehydration).
  • Sight: Assess for normal sight, impaired sight, use of glasses, squinting, or loss of eye/blindness.
  • Conjunctiva and sclera: Normal conjunctiva is clear, moist, and smooth. Abnormal findings include ecchymosis, redness, jaundice, excessive tearing or discharge, or hemorrhage from trauma.
  • Pupils: Pupils should be regular, equal, and respond to light. Normal pupils constrict quickly in direct response to light, and the opposite pupil should also constrict. Abnormal findings include dilated pupils (from trauma, neurologic disorders, glaucoma).
  • Eyelids: Check for redness, puffiness, or drooping (ptosis). Ptosis may be related to muscle weakness or neurologic lesion.
  • Eyebrows and eyelashes: Inspect for abnormalities.

  • Use a Snellen chart (contains various-sized letters with standardized numbers at the end of each line).
  • Position the chart 20 feet in front of the client.
  • Remove corrective lenses.
  • Instruct the client to cover one eye and read lines, starting with the top of the chart from left to right.
  • Note the line where the client correctly reads more than half the letters.
  • Record results as a fraction 20/distance number, and the number of letters missed for the eye test.
  • Repeat for the other eye.
  • Normal vision is 20/20 (at a distance of 20 feet, the normal eye can read the chart).

Ear Assessment (ØªÙ‚ÙŠÙŠÙ… Ø§Ù„Ø£Ø°Ù†)

Introduction:

The ear is a sense organ of hearing and equilibrium. Assessment of hearing provides important information about the patient's ability to interact with the environment. To perform a thorough assessment of the ear, the nurse should have a good understanding of the external ear, the middle ear, and the inner ear.

Anatomy and Physiology:

The ears are located on either side of the head. The outer ear is positioned and shaped to help collect sound waves from the environment. The pinna is the external ear structure, comprising cartilage and skin. The ear canal begins at the external ear and ends at the tympanic membrane. The canal has many nerve fibers and ceruminous glands that produce cerumen (earwax). The tympanic membrane separates the external ear from the middle ear.

Physical Assessment:

  • Physical assessment of the ears consists of auditory screening, inspection and palpation of the external ear, and otoscopic assessment.
  • Observe the client for signs of hearing difficulty during the physical examination, such as turning the head, lipreading, and speaking in a loud voice.
  • If the client is wearing a hearing aid, ask if it is turned on.
  • Condition of ears: Normally, the ear canal is clean, dry, free from lesions, and with minimal amount of wax. If there is discharge, note color, amount, consistency, and clarity.
  • Inspection: Examine external ears for placement, symmetry, and color. Normal findings: Symmetrical, with upper attachment at level of eye's corner. Inspect the external ear for intactness, general hygiene, a buildup of wax, discharge, redness, and swelling. Redness indicates inflammation or fever. Inspect the external auditory canal using an otoscope. Note any discharge along with the color and consistency of the cerumen (earwax). Normal cerumen is yellow or orange and soft, moist. Abnormal findings include bloody or purulent discharge (may be related to otitis media) or bloody or watery drainage (may indicate leakage of cerebrospinal fluid or skull trauma).

Hearing and Equilibrium Tests:

  • Whisper test: To assess a patient's "gross" hearing. Instruct the patient to occlude one ear, then stand 1-2 feet away and whisper a two-digit number with your mouth covered. Test both ears. Normal finding: Patient correctly repeats the two-syllable word. Abnormal finding: Inability to hear words may indicate high-frequency hearing loss (e.g., from excessive exposure to loud noises).
  • Weber test: Strike a tuning fork against your fist or pinch the prongs together. Hold the base of the vibrating fork with your thumb and index finger and place the base on the center of the top of the client's head. Ask the client to describe the sound. Normally, sound is heard equally in both ears (a "negative" Weber test).
